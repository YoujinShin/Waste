Tracking e-waste smuggling routes across the world


"This project is part of our continuous commitment to explore how sensing and other digital technologies can help us build a more just and equitable world.", Carlo Ratti, MIT Senseable City Lab

A joint project between the BAN and the MIT Senseable City Lab has led to the discovery of previously unknown international electronic waste smuggling routes. Seven CRT monitors embedded with tracking devices were deposited at electronics recyclers in Los Angeles, California. These trackers revealed that five monitors were exported to Asia via ports in Vietnam, China, and Malaysia.

CRTs are categorized as toxic waste due to the high content of lead in their glass, and they are the only e-waste export that the federal government restricts. Previous investigative reports by BAN and other groups have documented the export of e-waste to Asian and African ports, and pinpointed regions of Nigeria and China that had become toxic wastelands. However, many of the shipping routes and processing locations of CRTs and other e-waste remained a mystery.

The captured data indicate that five of the seven monitors were exported to Asia between August and November 2011. Entering through ports in Vietnam, China, and Malaysia, four devices ultimately settled in China, with the last ending up in Malaysia. This led to the discovery and documentation of a previously unknown smuggling route to e-waste recycling facilities in southern China, via the border region of northern Vietnam.

On-the-ground investigations by BAN produced a clearer picture of these trade routes. E-waste enters the country in inter-modal containers through the port of Haiphong. Locals transfer the containers to semi trucks, which transport them 260km to the border town of Mong Cai on the Ka Long River. Interviews with locals and eyewitness reports revealed that small riverboats smuggle an array of goods, from e-waste and narcotics to endangered species and women, from Mong Cai to the Chinese border town of Dongxing.
